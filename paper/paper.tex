\begin{abstract}
Arabic text correction is a difficult problem in Arabic natural language processing because the language exhibits rich orthographic variation, visually confusable characters, and inconsistent surface forms in real-world data sources such as OCR, speech transcription, and noisy user input. In this domain, even small character-level errors can change meaning, break token boundaries, or reduce grammatical fluency, which makes correction a nontrivial processing task rather than a simple spelling cleanup. This paper presents an end-to-end correction framework with two complementary operating modes: a lightweight character-level encoder-decoder Transformer trained on synthetic noisy-clean Arabic pairs, and a deployment-oriented correction service built on the pre-trained CAMeL-Lab AraBART model within a Streamlit application. The custom model is evaluated on an in-domain held-out synthetic test split and reaches 0.0364 CER, while the AraBART-based system is evaluated in a zero-shot deployment setting and provides stronger grammatical fluency and broader usability across manual text, files, OCR images, audio, and live speech. Rather than claiming a direct head-to-head winner, the study analyzes the trade-off between in-domain character-level fidelity and real-world, multi-modal robustness. The main contribution is a reproducible Arabic text-correction workflow that unifies data curation, normalization, controlled noise synthesis, model training, error analysis, and user-facing deployment.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Arabic text correction remains a high-impact problem for digital education, media quality control, accessibility tools, and content moderation systems. The task is difficult because Arabic writing contains orthographic variants, visually confusable characters, optional diacritics, and spacing patterns that can change meaning, while OCR and speech transcription may introduce broken words, missing characters, merged tokens, or repeated substitutions. As a result, Arabic correction must be treated as a structured language-processing problem at both the character level and the sequence level.

Neural sequence modeling has progressed rapidly from recurrent encoder-decoder models to attention-based Transformers and large pre-trained language models \cite{sutskever2014,bahdanau2015,vaswani2017,devlin2019}. This progress makes text correction a generation problem, but it also raises a practical question: should an Arabic correction system prioritize a compact task-specific model or a larger pre-trained model with stronger linguistic priors? In this paper, we address that trade-off with two complementary solutions: a compact custom Transformer for controlled character-level correction and a pre-trained AraBART-based service for deployment-oriented multi-modal correction.

This paper fills a gap in how Arabic correction systems are often presented by combining the data pipeline, synthetic noise generation, model training, evaluation, and user-facing deployment in one reproducible workflow. The study introduces a compact character-level Transformer trained with scheduled sampling and mixed precision, alongside a Streamlit-based correction service built on CAMeL-Lab AraBART, and evaluates both paths through the lens of trade-offs in CER, BLEU, and qualitative error behavior across in-domain and zero-shot deployment conditions.

\section{Literature Review}
\subsection{Seq2Seq Foundations and Transformer-Based Correction}
Sequence-to-sequence models framed text correction as conditional generation from noisy to fluent text \cite{sutskever2014,bahdanau2015}. Attention and Transformer architectures improved this setup by modeling long-range dependencies that are critical for spelling and spacing recovery \cite{vaswani2017}. Pre-trained denoising models such as BART and T5 further demonstrated that corruption-and-reconstruction objectives transfer effectively to correction tasks \cite{lewis2020bart,raffel2020t5}. These foundations support the current study's generation-based view of Arabic correction rather than rule-only character replacement.

\subsection{Arabic-Specific Pre-Training and Error-Correction Benchmarks}
Arabic correction is harder than many high-resource settings because morphology and orthographic variation increase ambiguity at both token and character levels. Arabic-focused pre-training (e.g., AraBERT and CAMeL-related efforts) has shown clear gains over generic multilingual baselines for downstream understanding and generation tasks \cite{antoun2020arabert,inoue2021camel}. For evaluation, QALB remains the principal benchmark for Arabic error correction \cite{rozovskaya2014qalb,rozovskaya2015qalb}, while recent neural approaches (BiLSTM and T5 variants) report progress but still exhibit weaknesses under heavy noise and confusable forms \cite{abandah2022bilstm,qaraghuli2024t5}. This motivates comparing a compact controlled model with a stronger pre-trained Arabic model under distinct operating conditions.

\subsection{Deployment-Oriented Arabic Correction Systems}
Practical Arabic correction systems require more than model accuracy; they must also integrate OCR, speech recognition, and responsive serving workflows. Whisper and OCR services make multi-modal text acquisition feasible in production pipelines \cite{radford2023whisper,ocrspace}, while PyTorch and Transformers reduce deployment overhead for generation models \cite{paszke2019pytorch,wolf2020transformers}. Existing Arabic studies mostly emphasize benchmark scores, with fewer end-to-end reports that unify text, OCR, and ASR correction in one user-facing application. This gap directly motivates the deployment-oriented contribution of the present work.

\section{Methods}
\subsection{Data Acquisition and Curation}
The project begins with large-scale Arabic news collection from Youm7. A dedicated scraping pipeline was implemented with asynchronous requests, retry handling, and polite randomized delays to reduce server pressure and improve robustness of collection. Raw pages then passed through multi-stage cleaning to remove boilerplate artifacts (menus, ads, repeated widgets) and retain article body text only.

After cleaning and quality filtering, the corpus reaches 100{,}000 articles, from which 10{,}000 noisy-clean pairs are prepared for controlled model development. The paired set is split into 8{,}000 training pairs, 1{,}000 validation pairs, and 1{,}000 held-out test pairs. Articles shorter than a minimum content threshold were discarded, and samples with excessive non-Arabic content were excluded to preserve linguistic consistency. The pipeline also stores provenance metadata (source URL and publication date) to support traceability and future auditing.

This design supports reproducible experimentation because the noise-injection process is explicit and therefore easier to analyze than uncontrolled production noise. \subsection{Arabic Normalization and Noise Injection}
\label{sec:noise_injection}
A deterministic Arabic normalization stage standardizes Alef variants, Yaa variants, Hamza forms, and Teh Marbuta behavior, while removing tatweel and diacritics where needed. Synthetic noise is then injected at character and punctuation levels to approximate realistic errors from typing, OCR, and ASR. During synthesis, each sample is corrupted with an overall corruption budget of 20\%, decomposed into 10\% substitutions, 5\% deletions, and 5\% insertions. Substitutions are drawn from a predefined confusable-character map (including Alef/Hamza and Yaa/Alif-Maqsura variants), with additional keyboard-adjacency and punctuation changes. This explicit policy enables reproducible corruption and allows targeted analysis of frequent Arabic confusion classes.

To improve realism beyond random perturbation, the synthetic generator groups errors into practical categories observed in noisy Arabic writing: confusable-letter substitutions, Hamza/Alef form swaps, Teh Marbuta--Heh alternation, punctuation drift, spacing boundary shifts, dropped characters, and short insertion bursts. This category-based design keeps the corruption process analyzable while still producing diverse patterns.

Fig.~\ref{fig:project_pipeline} is the central figure of this paper because it unifies the complete research lifecycle in one reproducible process rather than presenting model training and deployment as disconnected tasks. The pipeline should be read from left to right. First, raw Arabic news content is collected and filtered to establish a high-quality clean-text base. Second, deterministic normalization is applied to reduce orthographic variability (Alef, Hamza, Yaa, Teh Marbuta, tatweel, and diacritics handling), ensuring consistent character space before corruption. Third, synthetic noise injection generates supervised noisy-clean pairs under controlled corruption rates, producing a transparent and auditable training signal for character-level correction.

The workflow then splits into two complementary branches with different scientific purposes. Branch A is the in-domain modeling branch: the custom seq2seq Transformer is trained on the synthetic pairs, validated on held-out data, and analyzed with character-level diagnostics (CER distributions and confusion patterns). This branch provides tight experimental control and interpretable error analysis. Branch B is the deployment branch: the pre-trained AraBART model is integrated into a user-facing Streamlit system that accepts heterogeneous inputs (manual text, uploaded files, OCR images, audio files, and live speech). This branch evaluates practical robustness and usability under realistic conditions where input noise is not distribution-matched to the synthetic generator.

Taken together, the figure formalizes the main contribution of the study as a two-tier architecture: a controlled benchmarking path for method analysis and a real-world serving path for application utility. This design also clarifies why results are reported in separated evaluation settings: each branch optimizes a different objective (in-domain reconstruction fidelity versus cross-modal deployment readiness), and both are required for a complete Arabic correction framework.

\subsection{Custom Transformer Model}

\begin{figure}[!t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{Diagrams/seq2seq.png}
\caption{Custom seq2seq Transformer used in Branch A. Noisy Arabic character sequences are embedded and position-encoded, processed by stacked encoder self-attention layers, and decoded autoregressively with causal masking and encoder-decoder attention to produce corrected output characters.}
\label{fig:transformer_blocks}
\end{figure}

Fig.~\ref{fig:transformer_blocks} is placed here to ground the model description before listing hyperparameters. The figure explains how noisy Arabic character sequences are encoded into contextual representations and then decoded autoregressively into corrected text, including encoder context building, decoder-side causal generation, and attention-based conditioning on the source sequence.

The custom model is a character-level seq2seq Transformer with embedding dimension 256, 3 encoder layers, 3 decoder layers, 8 attention heads, feed-forward dimension 512, dropout 0.1, and maximum sequence length 128. Training uses Adam \cite{kingma2015adam} with learning rate $10^{-4}$, scheduled sampling, mixed precision, and checkpointing. This configuration intentionally favors compactness and interpretability over maximum scale, which is appropriate for analyzing correction behavior at the character level.

\subsection{Pre-trained LLM Correction Service}
For deployment-grade correction, the project uses the Hugging Face model \url{CAMeL-Lab/arabart-qalb15-gec-ged-13}. This path is integrated into a Streamlit application and supports manual text entry, text-file upload, image OCR via the OCR.space API, audio upload via Whisper, and live recording. The deployment layer is therefore not a separate demonstration artifact; it is a functional extension of the modeling pipeline that allows the same correction logic to be exercised across multiple real-world input modalities.

\section{Experiments}
\subsection{Metrics}
The primary metric is Character Error Rate (CER), defined as:
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{CER}=\frac{S+D+I}{N},
\end{equation}
where $S$ is substitutions, $D$ deletions, $I$ insertions, and $N$ is reference length in characters. CER is paired with BLEU for broader assessment of lexical and sequence quality \cite{levenshtein1966,papineni2002bleu}.

\subsection{Training Protocol}
The custom model is trained for 10 epochs with batch size 32 on GPU. Scheduled sampling decays the teacher forcing ratio from 1.0 to 0.3 across epochs. Mixed precision is enabled to reduce memory cost and improve throughput. The project logs loss and token-level accuracy per epoch, and stores checkpoints for reproducibility. This protocol reflects a deliberate trade-off between model stability and exposure to imperfect generated context, which is especially relevant for sequence-correction tasks.

For the application path, inference uses the pre-trained AraBART model through Hugging Face pipelines, with OCR input through OCR.space and speech input through Whisper \cite{camel_arabart,ocrspace,radford2023whisper}. The two paths are evaluated separately so that the custom Transformer can be read as a controlled synthetic-noise model while AraBART can be interpreted as a deployment-oriented correction service.

\subsection{Evaluation Procedure}
The evaluation procedure is intentionally separated into two settings. For in-domain benchmarking, the custom Transformer is evaluated on the held-out synthetic test split of 1{,}000 noisy-clean pairs generated with the same corruption policy described in Section~\ref{sec:noise_injection}. For deployment-oriented assessment, AraBART is evaluated in a zero-shot setting on application-style inputs (manual text, OCR outputs, and ASR transcripts) that are not distribution-matched to the synthetic training generator. Consequently, Table~\ref{tab:combined_results} should be read as a side-by-side summary of two operating regimes rather than a strict like-for-like leaderboard.

\subsection{Baselines}
To contextualize model behavior, the evaluation design includes two reference baselines for the synthetic test split: (1) a normalization-only identity baseline (input passes through normalization without neural correction), and (2) a dictionary-driven Arabic spell-correction baseline (Hunspell/Farasa-style lexical correction when available). These baselines provide lower-complexity anchors for interpreting neural gains and should be reported alongside neural metrics in the final camera-ready version.

In addition, exploratory project runs used sequence-model and rule-based references to situate expected performance ranges for Arabic noisy-text correction. \section{Results and Discussion}
\subsection{Training Dynamics and Convergence}
The training trajectory demonstrates rapid convergence in the early epochs and then a gradual flattening as optimization approaches its best operating point. Accuracy increases from 38.38\% in epoch 1 to 91.69\% by epoch 6, after which performance remains high but begins to soften slightly, reaching 89.36\% by epoch 10. This pattern is consistent with a model that learns the dominant character regularities quickly and then starts to overfit to the synthetic noise distribution later in training. In practical terms, the learning curve indicates that the architecture is sufficiently expressive for the task, but that additional regularization or earlier stopping could potentially preserve the best generalization point more effectively.

\subsection{Quantitative Comparison of the Two Correction Paths}
Table~\ref{tab:combined_results} presents in-domain model benchmarking and deployment-oriented system profiling in a single side-by-side view. The custom Transformer column reports controlled synthetic-split results (CER and BLEU), whereas the AraBART column reports zero-shot deployment characteristics for heterogeneous real-world modalities. The training-time contrast remains meaningful: the custom model is cheaper to deploy after training, while the pre-trained model is faster to adopt but heavier to serve.

\subsection{Error Distribution and Character Confusions}
Error analysis shows that substitutions dominate, accounting for roughly 60--70\% of observed mistakes, while insertions and deletions each contribute approximately 15--20\%. This distribution is typical of character-level Arabic correction because many of the relevant errors involve visually similar letters, orthographic variants, or spacing irregularities rather than complete word omissions. The concentration of errors in substitutions suggests that the model is often choosing between near-equivalent characters, which is precisely the kind of ambiguity that character-level modeling must resolve.

The confusion profile is concentrated around high-frequency characters and whitespace interactions. This outcome is consistent with Arabic writing conventions, where spacing can change token boundaries and several letters have close visual or contextual relationships. In particular, Alef and Hamza variants (e.g., \ar{ا}, \ar{أ}, \ar{إ}), and Yaa/Alif-Maqsura style confusions (\ar{ي}/\ar{ى}), remain difficult because they are semantically and orthographically close while also being sensitive to local context. Teh Marbuta and Heh confusions (\ar{ة}/\ar{ه}) are also recurrent in noisy text. For readability, a phrase-level Arabic example is shown here: \ar{اللغة العربية وتصحيح النصوص}. Whitespace errors are additionally amplified by character-level autoregressive decoding: once a boundary token is misplaced, subsequent characters may drift into shifted token boundaries, compounding local alignment errors. These observations indicate that the current augmentation strategy captures general corruption patterns well but still leaves room for targeted enrichment around the most problematic character groups.

The longer right tail corresponds to more severely corrupted samples, where multiple character-level errors accumulate and the model must rely on broader context to recover the intended sequence. Such cases are expected to remain difficult because the problem becomes increasingly underdetermined as noise density rises.

The shape of the distribution also clarifies the practical reliability of the system. A sharp peak around low error values implies that the model is well aligned with the dominant data regime, while the tail indicates that the model still has identifiable failure modes under heavier corruption. This is a useful outcome for deployment because it means the model is dependable for the majority of realistic inputs, but it also exposes a clear target for future refinement through stronger augmentation, curriculum learning, or more aggressive regularization.

Fig.~\ref{fig:char_confusion} presents a character-level confusion matrix for the most frequent symbols. The strong diagonal concentration confirms that the model has learned the core identity mapping for the majority of characters, which is the expected baseline for a correction system. The remaining off-diagonal regions are more informative than the diagonal itself because they reveal which substitutions continue to be difficult after training. In this study, the most visible confusions are associated with whitespace and with Arabic letters that are visually or orthographically close. The confusion matrix is therefore not only a descriptive figure but also a diagnostic tool. It identifies the most persistent misprediction pairs and suggests where targeted improvements should be applied. For example, synthetic augmentation can be biased toward Alef and Hamza contrasts, spacing normalization can be refined for boundary-sensitive cases, and the training objective can be adjusted to penalize specific classes of confusion more heavily. In this sense, the confusion analysis links the aggregate evaluation metrics to the concrete mechanisms of model failure and provides a practical roadmap for future refinement.

\subsection{Implications for Deployment}
Taken together, the results suggest that the project works best as a two-tier correction system: the custom Transformer serves as a compact, domain-aligned model, and AraBART serves as a more fluent deployment layer for heterogeneous real-world inputs. The error analysis also shows that future improvements should prioritize whitespace handling and Alef/Hamza-style confusions rather than broader architectural expansion. A direct fair-comparison benchmark between both models on a shared external dataset (e.g., QALB) remains an essential next experimental step.

\section{Conclusion}
This paper presented a reproducible Arabic text-correction workflow spanning corpus collection, normalization, synthetic noise generation, model training, evaluation, and deployment. A compact character-level Transformer and a pre-trained AraBART service were developed to represent the two main operating points for Arabic correction: controlled benchmarking and practical user-facing deployment.

The results indicate that the compact model is strong on the project-specific synthetic regime, while the pre-trained service is better suited to fluency-oriented correction across manual text, files, OCR, audio, and live speech. The remaining errors are concentrated in whitespace handling and Alef/Hamza-related confusions, suggesting that future work should focus on targeted augmentation, stronger regularization, earlier stopping, inclusion of classical rule-based baselines, and validation on shared external Arabic correction benchmarks.
